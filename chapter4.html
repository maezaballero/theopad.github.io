Chapter Four

Conversation

MZ 6.7K views

The balcony was quiet.

Most of the noise from the event stayed inside. Out here, it was just the two of us, the evening breeze, and the view of the school grounds below.

Mae stood there near the railing, looking out. Not at anything in particular.

I stood a few feet away, trying to find the right place to start. I followed my instinct to get here, but I didn't really know what would happen next. I had no idea how this was supposed to go. I only knew that I held on to these observations around for weeks now. After tonight, after watching that expression on Mae's face when the audio broke down, something in me decided to get to the bottom of this.

"So," Mae said before I could begin. "What did you want to talk about?"

The voice was steady, as expected. Casual, almost. As if we were about to discuss something ordinary like an assignment.

I looked at Mae directly.

"How are you doing?" I asked. "And I don't mean tonight. I mean in general."

A pause.

"I'm fine," Mae said with the same steady delivery.

"Mae."

"I really am fine, Christine. It's been a bit busy these past weeks, but that's normal. Everything is under control."

"I saw your face when the audio broke down."

The atmosphere got a little quieter. Mae's hands, which was resting loosely on the bar, tightened their grasp.

"It was a minor technical issue," Mae said. "It was resolved."

"I know it was resolved. That's not what I'm talking about."

Mae turned to look at me then. Not with irritation. Mae looked more like someone who just realized the conversation was going somewhere they hadn't prepared for.

"Then what are you talking about?" Mae asked.

"I've been noticing things," I said. "For a while now. Since the assembly, actually. Since before that, maybe."

"Noticing what?"

"The way you go quiet mid-conversation. The way you grasp on to things when you talk about your responsibilities. The way you smiled after the speech even though I could tell something was off. The notes you left on that paper."

Mae suddenly went very still.

"You read that note?" The voice was quieter now. Not angry. More like exposed.

"It was on the table. I didn't mean to." I stated in my defense. "But I did. And I haven't been able to stop thinking about it since."

For about a minute, neither of us said anything.

"Those were just some personal reminders," Mae said. "I have high standards for myself. That's not a bad thing."

"No one said it was."

"Then I don't understand what the problem is."

I looked at Mae carefully. "The problem is that unacceptable and no second chances doesn't sound like someone who has high standards. They sound like someone who isn't allowed to make mistakes. And those are two very different things."

Mae looked away, gazing the school grounds below.

"You're thinking too much about it," Mae said.

"Maybe," I said, then I firmly replied. "But I don't think I am."

Another silence between us. This one felt different. Less like a pause and more like something being held back.

I waited.

"You don't know what it's like...," Mae said. The voice still felt controlled, but something behind it changed. "...to be in this position. People look at you a certain way. They expect certain things. And once you've been that person for long enough, you can't just... stop performing. You don't get a rest day. You don't get to make mistakes. Because if you do, everything you've worked for starts to fall apart."

"That sounds tiring," I said quietly.

"It's just who I am now."

"Is it? Or is it something you've convinced yourself of?"

Mae turned to look at me again, and I felt that there was something sharper in the expression.

"I've worked hard for everything that I have," Mae said. "The positions. The recognitions. None of it came for free. I earned all of it, and I intend to keep it that way."

"I'm not questioning that," I said. "I know that you work hard. I've seen it. But working hard and running yourself into the ground are not the same thing."

"I'm not running myself into the ground."

"Then why do you look like you haven't actually rested well in a while?"

Mae didn't answer right away. And that, more than anything else, told me what I needed to know.

"I rest," Mae said, eventually. "I manage my time."

"Managing time isn't the same as actually resting. And from what I've seen, you don't stop. Even now when the event is technically over, you were still stacking chairs with the teachers."

"Someone had to."

"The teachers were already doing it."

Mae looked away again.

I softened my voice. "I'm not trying to berate you or anything. I'm genuinely asking. When was the last time you let yourself just... relax? Live your life? Without something to do or something to check or something to be responsible for?"

Mae had no answer.

That was Mae's answer.

"Look," Mae said, after a moment. "I appreciate that you're concerned. I do. But this is just how things are. Some people are just built for this kind of pressure. I've always been like this. It's fine."

"The words on that paper didn't look fine."

Mae tightened. "That was private."

"I know. And I'm sorry for bringing it up. But no second chances? Is that really how you talk to yourself? Is that the standard you're holding yourself to?"

"It keeps me on top of things."

"More like keeping you afraid of things."

Silence.

For a moment, I thought Mae was going to end the conversation here and now.

"I'm not afraid," Mae said, quietly.

"Not of acads," I said. "But of what people would think if they found out you were struggling. That's different."

Mae was quiet for a long time.

When the answer finally came, it was slow and careful.

"You don't understand the environment we're in, Christine. These people have a certain image of me. And if that image cracks even a little, it changes the entire picture. It changes how my peers see me. How the batches below me see me. Some things are better kept to yourself."

"So you just carry it alone."

"Everyone carries some things alone."

"Not everyone has to."

Mae looked at me for just a moment, and in that moment, I caught something. A hidden expression. Tired, real, and carefully hidden.

"You're the first person who actually noticed," Mae said, slowly.

"And that's the problem," Mae continued. "If someone notices, then it becomes a thing. And if it becomes a thing, then people start asking questions. And I can't afford for people to start asking questions right now. I have too much to maintain."

"So instead you just keep going until something breaks?"

"Nothing is going to break."

"Mae."

"Christine." The voice was gentle but firm. "I understand what you're saying. I really do. But I'm used to managing this for a long time already. I don't need anyone to step in and fix something that isn't broken. I have everything under control."

I looked at Mae for a long moment.

"Okay," I said.

"Okay?"

"I'm not going to push you. But I want you to know that if something ever does feel like too much, or if you ever want to actually talk about it, I'm always here." I paused. "No conditions. No judgement."

Something changed in Mae's expression. Not quite relief, but something similar to it. As if a small unravelling of a facade that was there a long time.

"Noted," Mae said.

Noted.

We stood there for another moment. We were just two people on the balcony at the end of a long night, not quite finished but not quite sure how to keep going.

"You should go back to your friends," Mae said eventually. "They're probably waiting."

"They are," I agreed.

The smallest smile crossed Mae's face. Genuine, this time. Small, but there.

"Go," Mae said.

I stepped back from the balcony. Then I stopped.

"Mae," I said.

"Yes?"

"From what I see, tonight was a success. The whole event. Everyone had a great time. One minor hiccup doesn't change that."

Mae didn't respond immediately. Mae just looked out at the school grounds again.

"Goodnight, Christine," Mae said.

"Goodnight."

· · ·

I went home that night replaying the conversation over and over in my mind.

Mae listened. Even in small ways, Mae opened up a little. Enough to say: you're the first person who has actually noticed. Enough to let a little of the weight show through.

But then the wall had gone back up.

I have everything under control.

I didn't believe that entirely. And I wasn't sure Mae did either.

But I also understood that you cannot reach someone by force. You cannot pull a person out of the place they built for themselves just because you could see it clearly from the outside. They need to be willing to step toward the door first.

All you could do was make sure they knew the door existed.

And that someone was standing on the other side of it, waiting.
