Chapter Three

Halloween Night

Ijbol67 67K views

Many weeks passed since that meeting after the assembly.

Somewhere between then and now, Mae and I started talking more regularly. It wasn't anything dramatic or sudden. It happened gradually. A greeting in the hallway. A short exchange near the canteen. Mae passing by our classroom and stopping for a moment to wave at me.

Once or twice a week, we would end up talking. Sometimes it was short. Other times it stretched a little longer, the kind of conversation that didn't have a clear ending point but eventually arrived at one anyway when the bell rang.

It was Mae who offered to help me with my studies first.

I hadn't even asked. But one afternoon, Mae apparently noticed me struggling with a particularly difficult set of mathematical factoring problems at one of the tables in the library, and simply sat down across me and said, "Do you want help with that?"

I said yes before I could even think about it.

And that was how it started.

Mae was patient in a way that surprised me. Not the kind of patient that felt like someone was waiting for you to catch up, but the kind that made you feel like there was no rush, like the problem in front of you was solvable and the only thing needed was time. Mae had a way of explaining things that made them feel easier and more digestible.

I understood now why everyone praised Mae the way they did.

I could see it clearly. The competence. The composure. The almost effortless way of meeting the demands of academics. Mae was the kind of person who made excellence look habitual. The kind of person our teachers mention as a model student to the lower batches of students.

And yet.

I still could not let go of what I saw after the assembly.

I told myself it was nothing. People get bad moments at some point. Notes on that paper were just the habits of someone with high standards. That was normal, wasn't it? To want to do better? To hold yourself accountable?

Maybe.

But the words stuck in my mind. Unacceptable. Do better next time. No second chances. Not a reminder. Something else. Something heavier than that.

There were other small things I started noticing too now that we talked more regularly.

Mae would sometimes go quiet mid-conversation before snapping back with a smile. Mae would mention a upcoming responsibility or obligation, and the words would come out steady and certain, while the hands would find something to hold, like a pen or the edge of a notebook. Mae laughed at the right moments but never quite let it reach full volume.

I noticed. I always seemed to notice.

I didn't say anything about any of it. It wasn't my place. Not yet.

But I kept my observations quietly. Observations kept off to the side, waiting.

· · ·

The Halloween Music Showcase finally arrived on the last Friday of October, right before the start of our semestral break.

The school was preparing for it for weeks. Audition posters were posted along the main corridor sometime in early October. After that, everything took off. Students began signing up for solo performances. Band lineups were formed and adjusted.

The venue was the AVR and covered court, which was transformed for the evening. The teachers were decorating the whole day, since classes were suspended to make way for the event. Orange and black streamers hanged along the hallway. Boxes and caution tape were placed at the lobby. A fog machine was set up near the stage entrance which the younger students kept playing with.

I arrived with Bea and Lara sometime between 6 and 7, both of them already in costume. Bea came as a nurse, while Lara went as some pink-haired anime character.

"Christine, you really didn't dress up?" Bea said the moment she saw me.

"I'm dressed as a civilian."

"That's not a costume."

"It is if you believe."

Lara laughed. We found seats in the AVR among the front rows. Around us, the room was already filling up with students in varying degrees of costume, groups of friends taking their pictures, and the occasional teacher in an online-bought headband trying to look trendy.

The student council was easy to spot as they were wearing their council shirts. They were checking things, adjusting this-and-that, talking about something with the teachers.

As expected, at the center of it was Mae.

I watched from a distance as Mae managed the preparations. Mae was talking to the tech team, checking a clipboard, and helping a group of performers set up their instruments. It all looked perfect from my position. Mae had the kind of organized that didn't look like effort.

But I watched the pace of everything. The way Mae moved from one thing to the next without a pause in between. The way the clipboard was checked and rechecked. The way Mae swept through the venue, as if looking for something that hadn't gone wrong yet.

Mae was literally everywhere, which makes sense. Mae was one of the event heads after all. But still, how does one person hold all of that together?

I didn't have an answer. I was still watching.

· · ·

The performances began. There were solo acts, a few small bands, one group performance from the upper batch that brought great applause. Mae really did a great job with selecting the performers.

It was during the intermission, somewhere between the 6th and 7th performers, that something went wrong.

I almost missed it. I stepped away briefly and was on my way back when I heard a noise near the stage.

The audio system broke down.

Not completely, though. Someone tripped over one of the wires at the wrong moment, and in the rush to fix it, the wrong plug had been inserted, creating a buzzing noise that spread across in the room.

It was the kind of technical failure that normally happens at school events. These things happen.

But Mae was the one who was responsible in assisting the performers set up.

I watched from across the room as Mae moved quickly to the solve the problem. The fix came within a few minutes. The proper plug was inserted and the buzzing noise disappeared.

Most people probably didn't even think of it as a failure. Just a brief hiccup in the transition to the next performers.

The succeeding performers continued on without any error.

But I saw Mae's face in the moment the buzzing noise hit.

Not panic. Not anger. Something quieter, and somehow worse. The kind of expression that didn't have a name but that I recognized anyway. It was the look of someone doing some mental calculations and arriving at an answer they didn't like.

Mae stayed by the stage for the rest of the performances. Not because it was necessary. The issue was already resolved, but Mae stayed anyway, watching the whole time.

Even from a distance, I could see the difference in Mae's posture from earlier. Mae had the same composure on the surface, but something underneath it tightened.

The performances ended just before nine. Afterwards, the crowd went out in covered court, eager to have some snacks and talk to their friends.

For most, the evening was a success. The audience loved it. The performances were great. The technical issue was handled and forgotten.

I should have gone home with Bea and Lara.

They were already waiting near the exit. I told them to give me a minute.

I found Mae near the stage area helping with the cleanup. The clipboard was gone now. Mae was quietly stacking chairs with the teachers, accomplishing chores in silence while everyone else talked around the event.

I waited until the other students moved to a different part of the room.

"Mae," I called out.

Mae looked up.

"Can I speak with you for a minute? In private."

A brief expression went up on Mae's face. Then the calm, composed expression settled back into place. The glass wall back up where it always was.

"Sure," Mae said, after stacking up the last chair.

We stepped away, just outside towards the balcony that overlooked the school grounds.

I looked at Mae.

I still wasn't sure exactly what I was going to say.

But I knew I wasn't going to look away this time.
