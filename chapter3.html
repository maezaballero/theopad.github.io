Chapter Three

Halloween Night

MZ 6.7K views

Many weeks had passed since that meeting after the assembly.

Somewhere between then and now, Mae and I had started talking more regularly. It wasn't anything dramatic or sudden. It happened gradually, as way most things happen in school. A greeting in the hallway here. A short exchange near the bulletin board there. Mae passing by our classroom and stopping for a moment to see me.

Once or twice a week, we would end up talking. Sometimes it was short. A question about a school event. A comment about one of my projects. Other times it stretched a little longer, the kind of conversation that didn't have a clear ending point but eventually arrived at one anyway when the bell rang.

It was Mae who offered to help me with my studies first.

I hadn't even asked. But one afternoon, Mae had apparently noticed me struggling with a particularly difficult set of mathematical factoring problems at one of the tables in the library, and simply sat down across from me and said, "Do you want help with that?"

I said yes before I could even think about it.

And that was how it started.

Mae was patient in a way that surprised me. Not the kind of patient that felt like someone was waiting for you to catch up, but the kind that made you feel like there was no rush, like the problem in front of you was solvable and the only thing needed was time. Mae had a way of explaining things that made them feel easier and more digestible. Breaking them down. Laying the pieces out in order.

I understood now why everyone praised Mae the way they did.

I could see it clearly. The competence. The composure. The almost effortless way of meeting the demands of academics. Mae was the kind of person who made excellence look habitual. The kind of person our teachers mention as a model student to the lower batches of students.

And yet.

I still could not let go of what I had seen after the assembly.

I tried. I told myself it was nothing. People get bad moments at some point. Notes on that paper were just the habits of someone with high standards. That was normal, wasn't it? To want to do better? To hold yourself accountable?

Maybe.

But the words had stuck in my mind. Unacceptable. Do better next time. No second chances. Not a reminder. Not a note for improvement. Something else. Something heavier than that.

There were other small things I had started noticing too now that we talked more regularly.

Mae would sometimes go quiet mid-conversation before snapping back with a smile that came just a half-second too late. Mae would mention a upcoming responsibility or obligation, and the words would come out steady and certain. But the hands would find something to hold, like a pen or the edge of a notebook. Mae laughed at the right moments but never quite let it reach full volume.

I noticed. I always seemed to notice.

I didn't say anything about any of it. It wasn't my place. Not yet.

But I kept my observations quietly. Observations kept off to the side, waiting.

· · ·

The Halloween Music Showcase finally arrived on the last Friday of October, right before the start of our semestral break.

The school had been preparing for it for weeks. Audition posters had gone up along the main corridor sometime in early October. After that, everything took off. Students began signing up for solo performances. Band lineups were formed and adjusted. The energy had been climbing steadily, and by the time the actual event day came, it had reached a particular level of both excitement and chaos.

The venue was the AVR and covered court, which was transformed for the evening. The teachers had been decorating the whole day, since classes were suspended to make way. Orange and black streamers hanged along the hallway. Boxes and caution tape were placed at the lobby. A fog machine was set up near the stage entrance which the younger students kept playing with. The stage lights had been set up with proper lighting for once, after about two or three years of not working.

I arrived with Bea and Lara sometime between six and seven, both of them already in costume. Bea had come as a vampire, while Lara had gone as some pink-haired anime character.

"Christine, you really didn't dress up?" Bea said the moment she saw me.

"I'm dressed as a civilian."

"That's not a costume."

"It is if you believe."

Lara laughed. We found seats in the AVR among the front rows, a good angle for the stage. Around us, the room was already filling up with students in varying degrees of costume, clusters of friends taking group pictures, and the occasional teacher in an online-bought headband trying to look trendy.

The student council was easy to spot. They moved through the venue, while wearing their council shirts. They were checking things, adjusting this-and-that, talking about something with the teachers. Basically, they were doing event coordination in real time.

As expected, at the center of it was Mae.

I watched from a distance as Mae managed the preparations. Mae was talking to the tech team, checking a clipboard, and helping a group of performers set up their instruments. It all looked perfect from my position. Mae had the kind of organized that didn't look like effort.

But I watched the pace of everything. The way Mae moved from one thing to the next without a pause in between. The way the clipboard was checked and rechecked. The way swept through the venue, as if looking for something that hadn't gone wrong yet.

Mae was literally everywhere, which makes sense. Mae was one of the event heads after all. But still, how does one person hold all of that together?

I didn't have an answer. I was still watching.

· · ·

The performances began around 7:30 and moved along according the schedule. There were solo acts, a few small bands, one group performance from the upper batch that brought great applause. Mae really did a great job with selecting the performers.

It was during the intermission, somewhere between the sixth and seventh act, that something went wrong.

I almost missed it. I had stepped away briefly and was on my way back when I heard a noise near the stage.

The audio system had broken down.

Not completely, though. Someone tripped over one of the wires at the wrong moment, and in the rush to fix it, the wrong plug had been inserted, creating a buzzing noise that spread across all speakers in the room.

It was the kind of technical failure that happened at school events. These things happen.

But Mae was the one who had been responsible in assisting the performers set up.

I watched from across the room as Mae moved quickly to the solve the problem. The fix came within a few minutes. The proper plug was inserted and the buzzing noise disappeared.

Most people probably hadn't even thought of it as a failure. Just a brief pause in the transition to the next performers.

The succeeding performers took the stage without any error.

But I had seen Mae's face in the moment the buzzing noise hit.

Not panic. Not anger. Something quieter than both of those, and somehow worse. The kind of expression that didn't have a name but that I recognized anyway. It was the look of someone doing some mental calculations, thinking about what had gone wrong, and arriving at something they didn't like.

Mae stayed by the stage for the rest of that performances. Not because it was necessary. The issue had already been resolved. But Mae stayed anyway, watching and monitoring the whole time.

Even from a distance, I could see the difference in Mae's posture from earlier. Mae had the same composure on the surface, but something underneath it had tightened.

The performances ended just before nine. Afterwards, the crowd went out in covered court, eager to have some snacks and talk to their friends.

For most, the evening had been a success. The audience had loved it. The performances were great. The technical issue had been handled and forgotten.

I should have gone home with Bea and Lara.

They were already waiting near the exit. I had told them to give me a minute.

I found Mae near the stage area helping with the cleanup. The clipboard was gone now. Mae was quietly stacking chairs with the teachers, accomplishing chores in silence while everyone else talked around the event.

I waited until the other students had moved to a different part of the room.

"Mae," I called out.

Mae looked up.

"Can I speak with you for a minute? In private."

A brief expression went up on Mae's face. Then the calm, composed expression settled back into place. The glass wall back up where it always was.

"Sure," Mae said, after stacking up the last chair.

We stepped away, just outside towards the balcony that overlooks the school grounds.

I looked at Mae.

I still wasn't sure exactly what I was going to say.

But I knew I wasn't going to look away this time.
