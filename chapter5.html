Chapter Five

Christmas Party

MZ 6.7K views

Over a month went by after my conversation with Mae on that balcony.

We still had our regular study sessions in the library. Same table. Same routine. Mae would arrive first to prepare our review transes, while I would arrive a few minutes later. Comfortable. Familiar.

It was as if that night on the balcony never happened.

Mae never brought it up. Neither did I. I meant what I said. I wasn't going to push. So I kept my word, and I watched, and I waited, and I told myself that was enough for now.

Time moved quickly the way it always did near the end of a semester. Suddenly it was exam week.

Thanks to our study sessions, I came out of it well. High marks across all subjects, better than I expected. I was genuinely relieved.

Mae, predictably, came out of it the way Mae came out of everything.

In the hallway afterwards, I passed by Mae's classroom and saw some students gathered around, offering congratulations. Mae accepted each one with that same easy smile. Gracious. Warm. Completely composed.

To them, Mae is the perfect role model of a student.

But of course, if something is too good to be true, then it most likely is.

· · ·

The Christmas Party was announced the following week, after everyone fully settled from the exams. It was a school-wide affair this year, held in the AVR with a short program organized by the student council.

Which meant Mae was involved.

I found this out not because Mae told me directly, but because I noticed one of Mae's notes containing details about a raffle.

At our next study session, I mentioned it casually.

"You're helping organize the Christmas Party too?"

"Just coordinating a few things," Mae said, not looking up from the reviewer.

"With everything else you handle?"

"It's not that heavy."

I let it go. I learned by now how to time my questions.

· · ·

The party itself was on the last day of classes before the Christmas break.

The AVR was transformed again, this time with red and green strips of paper that clearly spent a lot of time putting up. The program ran smoothly: a short presentation from each batch, two games, and the raffle.

Mae was everywhere during the setup, but once the party started, Mae seamlessly became just another student enjoying it. Talking to people. Laughing at the right moments. The student council shirt was swapped out for something more casual and festive. If you didn't know what to look for, you would have no idea that Mae was the one making sure that the raffle tickets were numbered and accounted for.

Bea, Lara, and I found a good spot near one of the tables and settled in.

"Mae looks like they're having a great time," Lara observed at one point, looking across the room.

I nodded in response and turned to look.

Something, however, sat quietly in my mind the whole afternoon. I couldn't fully describe it.

Mae looked fine. But I have been watching long enough to know the difference between Mae being present and Mae performing presence. And something about the afternoon felt more like the latter.

It was near the end of the program when I noticed Mae slip out through the side door.

I waited a few minutes before following.

· · ·

I found Mae outside, around the corner from the entrance. Mae was not hiding, exactly. Just standing there, away from the music and the noise.

Mae saw me and looked up.

"Just getting some air," Mae said, before I could say anything.

"Okay," I said. "I'll get some air too."

Mae looked at me for a brief moment.

We stood there in the quiet.

"Long day?" I asked.

"Long semester," Mae said.

Mae said it lightly, almost like a joke. But it landed differently than a joke.

"Yeah," I said. "It was."

Mae didn't say anything else for a while. Neither did I.

"You did a good job with the party," I said eventually. "It looks great in there."

"The whole council did," Mae said.

"Sure. But you know what I mean."

Mae gave a small nod.

I looked at the side profile of Mae's face. The composure was still there. It was always there. But underneath it, in the way Mae's eyes stayed fixed on the middle distance, I could see it.

Still carrying the burden.

All of it, still, alone.

Our conversation on the balcony opened a small door. But Mae hadn't walked through it. Mae simply acknowledged that the door existed, and then stepped back to where it was safe, and continued on.

I hadn't expected one conversation to change everything. I knew better than that.

But standing here now, watching Mae retreat into the quiet in the middle of the Christmas Party, I understood something more clearly than I had before.

This wasn't just about high standards or a busy semester. This was something that had been going on for a long time. Something that Mae learned to carry so well that most people would never see it.

Mae needed more than a door being left open.

But I also knew I couldn't be the one to force it.

Not yet.

"Come back inside when you're ready," I said quietly. "No rush."

Mae looked at me briefly.

"Thanks, Christine," Mae said.

I went back inside.

The raffle was still ongoing. Bea won something and was showing it to Lara. I sat back into my seat and watched the rest of the program.

However, my mind was still outside.
