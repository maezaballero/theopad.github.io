Chapter Six

Enlightenment

Ijbol67 67K views

The Christmas break flew by quickly.

Two weeks of rest and family gatherings. It was good while it lasted.

As January came and our final term began, I found myself thinking about Mae again.

Not with the same restlessness I carried throughout last year. Something changed within me over the break. I spent enough time thinking about everything I observed: the notes, the balcony, the Christmas Party, Mae slipping away to be alone. Somewhere along the way it stopped feeling like a mystery to solve.

It started feeling like a person who was hurt.

And I approached it all wrong.

My previous attempts had good intentions. I knew that. But looking back, they also came with an agenda. I wanted Mae to admit something, to acknowledge what I could already see, to take the door I was offering and walk through it. I framed it as concern, but underneath it I was also pushing. Pressing. Waiting for a crack to appear so I could let myself in.

It wasn't compassion.

It was impatience disguised as care.

Mae built walls for a reason. And instead of respecting that, I have been trying to knock it down each time.

I decided to try something different this time around.

· · ·

I found Mae in the library as usual, on the same table as always, already set up with our review transes.

I sat down across from Mae. Took out my own things. Nothing unusual.

After a few minutes, I asked, "How was your break?"

"Good," Mae said. "Quiet. Yours?"

"Same. Ate too much food."

Mae smiled a bit. "Me too."

We went back to work.

A few minutes passed before I put down my notes and looked up.

"Mae," I said. "I want to say something, and I just need you to hear it. You don't have to reply."

Mae looked up.

"I think I was going about this the wrong way," I said. "Every time I tried to talk to you about how you're doing, it came with this goal. Like I needed you to admit something or say something specific. And that wasn't fair to you." I paused. "So I'm not here to do that today. I'm not here to push or confront or try to get you to open up before you're ready."

Mae was quiet.

"I'm just here," I said. "That's all. If you ever want to talk about anything, not just the heavy stuff, I'm here. And if you don't, that's okay too. But I don't want you to think that this comes with any conditions."

Silence followed.

Mae looked down for a while, then looked back up at me.

"Why do you care this much?" Mae asked. Genuinely.

I thought about it honestly before answering.

"Because I see you," I said. "Not as student council or varsity. Just you. And what I see is someone who works incredibly hard and never lets anyone close enough to help carry any of it. That's a lonely way to live. And you deserve better than that."

Mae didn't answer for a moment. The library was quiet around us, just the soft sound of pages and the distant noise of the hallway outside.

"People don't really talk about this kind of thing," Mae said at last. "Not here. If you're struggling, you just figure it out quietly and keep going. That's what everyone does."

"I know," I said. "And I think that's the problem."

Mae looked at me.

"There's this idea that asking for help means you can't handle things," I continued. "That struggling means you're weak. That if you're falling apart inside, the right thing to do is to hide it and just hold yourself together. But that's not strength. That's just suffering internally in a way that's more socially acceptable."

Something in Mae's expression shifted, as if a window started opening slightly after being shut closed for a long time.

"I don't really know how to do it differently," Mae said. "I have been doing it this way for so long. The version of me that people know is the only version I know how to be."

"That's okay," I said. "You don't need to figure it all out right now. You don't need to solve everything overnight. But try to start small. The next time something is too much, instead of writing no second chances on a piece of paper, just say to someone that it was hard. That's it. That's enough."

Mae was quiet after that.

"It sounds simple when you say it like that," Mae said.

"Most things do," I said. "The hard part is actually doing it."

"You're persistent, Christine.”

"More like consistent," I said.

This made Mae laugh.

"I'll try," Mae said.

Not noted this time.

I'll try.

"That's all I'm asking," I said.

We went back to review after that. The rest of the session continued without any issue.

When we packed up to leave, Mae stopped at the door and looked back at me.

"Thank you, Christine," Mae said. "Really."

"Anytime," I said. "I mean that."

Mae nodded once.

Then we parted ways down the corridor. Mae headed up the stairs, carrying something between us that wasn't there before.

Not a solution. Not a fix.

Just the beginning of something honest.

And for now, that was enough.
